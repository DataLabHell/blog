How DDEVD estimates extreme values — and recognizes when its results can be trusted

Rare events are often the most expensive ones. Extreme rainfall, material failure, or critical process deviations occur only rarely. That is precisely why the data you would actually need to assess their risk reliably tends to be missing.

Two new papers from Data Lab Hell take up this question. Both introduce the Data-Driven Extreme Value Distribution (DDEVD), an approach to extreme value estimation that does without parametric tail assumptions while mathematically deriving when its results are reliable.

In short: DDEVD estimates extreme values from small or non-stationary datasets without assuming a parametric tail distribution — and at the same time provides a mathematically derived criterion for when the estimate can be trusted.

Why this matters

Wherever rare, consequential events have to be assessed from sparse or non-stationary data, the same question arises — and it concerns a surprising range of fields: hydrology and climate research, natural hazards such as heavy rainfall, floods, or earthquakes, insurance and finance, epidemiology and public health, as well as the reliability of technical systems.

DDEVD grew out of a general methodological question and is therefore deliberately not tailored to any particular field of application. What connects these fields is not the industry but the structure of the problem: the critical case occurs so rarely that classical statistics reaches its limits — while the consequences are too large to leave it unquantified.

The real point

For us, though, the more interesting question is a different one: many methods can estimate extreme values. But can a method also recognize when its own estimate is still trustworthy at all?

DDEVD does not replace classical extreme value theory. It extends it by an aspect that is often decisive in many applications: the ability to judge when an estimate is reliable in the first place.

With rare events in particular, this is often more important than the estimate itself. Because the greatest risk does not arise where we know nothing — but where we mistake uncertainty for certainty.

The problem with classical extreme value theory

Classical extreme value theory is mathematically extraordinarily successful. Its strongest results, however, hold in the asymptotic regime: large samples, stable processes, and enough observations in the tail of the distribution. Its methodological repertoire — block maxima, peaks-over-threshold, fitting a parametric distribution family to the tail — is built for these conditions.

Many real datasets lie precisely outside this regime. Anyone working with rare events knows the situation: they are by definition under-sampled, the underlying processes or systems change over time, boundary conditions shift. You then force a tail assumption onto the data that you cannot really justify, and hope that the asymptotic argument somehow holds.

The idea: DDEVD

The Data-Driven Extreme Value Distribution (DDEVD) dispenses with this parametric tail assumption. Instead of modelling only the most extreme data points, it uses the information in all observations. These are aggregated metastatistically; from this, the underlying distribution is reconstructed using kernel density estimation. The result is an extreme value distribution whose tail develops from the data itself rather than from a pre-selected mathematical form.

That alone would already be a fully non-parametric approach. But DDEVD shows its real strength at two further points.

First, the decisive lever — the bandwidth — need not be chosen heuristically; it can be derived directly from the error structure of the method. For kernel-based methods this is exactly where things usually become messy; here it is cleanly determined (via the mean integrated squared error, MISE).

Second — and this is the part that interests us most — we prove a stability law. It specifies from what data situation onward the bandwidth optimization is well-defined at all and the result remains reliable. In doing so, the method carries its own quality criterion. It tells you not only what the estimated extreme value distribution is, but also whether the estimate can be trusted in the given data situation.

This is no cosmetic detail. A method that operates in the sparse, non-stationary regime must know when it reaches its limit — otherwise it produces its nicest, and most dangerous, numbers precisely where there is least to back them up.

The two papers

Both papers are available as preprints on arXiv and complement each other.

The first preprint (arXiv:2605.21416) is the mathematical foundation: the detailed derivation of the MISE, the optimal bandwidth derived from it, and the stability conditions for the optimization procedure. Anyone who wants to follow the full derivation will find everything here.

The second preprint (arXiv:2606.12174) places the method in a broader context: the metastatistical aggregation, the non-parametric tail estimation, and the stability criterion as a derived law, prepared for a computational-science audience. This paper has been submitted to a leading international journal.
