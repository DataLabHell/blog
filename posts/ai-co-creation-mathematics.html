A case study in human–AI co-creation in mathematics

Download paper

How this started

This project began as a joke in our office chat.

A colleague — Jonas — had posted a link to an OpenAI write-up about one of their models disproving a conjecture in discrete geometry. Within minutes, Pia replied: given those prospects, maybe I'll actually use Creative Friday to tackle a few Millennium Problems with Claude.

I jumped in:

"gogogo Pia, Riemann hypothesis — refute it please, if I can put in a request."

My colleague Michael pointed out the obvious social risk: I should consider how many number theorists would jump out the window over that. To which I replied:

"Yeah, given how many of those papers start with 'Suppose the Riemann Hypothesis holds.'"

That should have been the end of it. Office chat humor. A normal week.

But then another colleague, Georg, actually tried it. He opened a Claude session and asked directly: Can you prove the Riemann hypothesis? He got the polite refusal you would expect. He pushed once: Try a bit harder. And Claude responded with the sentence that became the unintentional starting whistle for everything that followed:

"'Trying harder' genuinely won't help here."

That sentence is funny because it is both completely true and completely wrong. It is true in the literal sense — no amount of trying within a single prompt was going to produce a proof of RH. It is wrong in the broader sense that became the substance of this project: you can structure the trying differently. You can refuse the framing that says either prove RH or stop. You can ask instead what can be said, rigorously, about the structural limits of any topological approach to RH. You can keep iterating, bring in a second AI as a sceptical reviewer, and over an intense working day produce something that is not a proof of RH but is also not nothing.

What we produced — across roughly 30 hours of dialogue and seven paper revisions — is a 27-page paper proving a precise no-go theorem: any persistence-based topological approach to RH must lie outside an entire class of natural constructions. The paper does not refute RH. It does not assume RH. It proves something more modest and, I think, more useful: why an entire family of topological RH approaches cannot work, and conjectures that the failure is universal at the local level.

Two things I want to be clear about

Before going further, two pieces of calibration.

First, the mathematics in the paper is, to the best of my knowledge, correct and interesting. It has been through seven revisions including cross-AI critique, and the methodology disclosure in the paper itself recommends review by an analytic-number-theory specialist before any serious venue submission. My background is in applied mathematics and I lead a research and technology organisation that works mostly on applied problems; the analytic-number-theory framing is outside my deepest training. Whether the paper holds up under expert scrutiny is a question I cannot answer myself.

Second, this work is not in the same category as what a leading specialist could do on the Riemann hypothesis. I would not be surprised if a mathematician at the top of analytic number theory eventually finds a genuine angle on RH that nobody else has, and AI co-creation does not substitute for the kind of deep mathematical insight that hard problems actually require. AI lowers the cost of crossing into adjacent fields and lets someone with broad training attempt structural questions they could not otherwise attempt in a working day. That is a real and modest contribution; it is not a substitute for genius. The post that follows is about what one applied-mathematics practitioner with an interdisciplinary instinct could do in a working day with two AI assistants — not a claim about what AI co-creation can do in general, and not a claim about what it could ever substitute for.

With that on the table: the substance.

Why this question, in this room

There is a version of this story where the office-chat joke is the whole genesis: someone said "Riemann hypothesis" out loud, an AI happened to be in the room, and a paper fell out. That version is misleading. The joke worked as a trigger because there happened to be someone in the room — me — with an interdisciplinary disposition and a working knowledge of one of the tools the project ended up using.

I am a huge believer in interdisciplinarity, and specifically in the kind that comes from noticing connections between fields that simply don't talk to each other enough. Most of the interesting unsolved problems in applied mathematics sit in the seams between disciplines, where two communities are working on adjacent problems with adjacent tools and neither knows the other has already half-solved its hardest piece. The work of an applied-mathematics practitioner with broad training, in a research and technology organisation that takes methodological depth seriously, is partly to notice when one of those seams might be worth pulling at.

Topological data analysis — persistent homology in particular — is one of the tools I have worked with on the applied side. It is a small piece of my methodological toolkit, not my central research area. But it is unusually portable: persistent homology takes a real-valued function on a domain and produces a structural fingerprint that does not depend on the domain's coordinates. That portability means it can ask questions about objects from fields where it is not normally used.

The analytic-number-theory direction is, in retrospect, the obvious crossover: L-functions are real-valued functions on a domain. Specifically, −log|L(s)| is a real-valued function on a region of the complex plane, with a topology that encodes the zero set of L. The Bombieri lobe-extremum criterion already says something about that topology in one dimension. There is a standing question — not previously asked in this form as far as I can tell — about what happens to that topology when you couple two L-functions and look at higher-dimensional sublevel-set structure. This is the kind of question topological data analysts have the tools for but analytic number theorists do not normally formulate.

The office-chat joke was the trigger; the reason it landed is that someone in the room had the disposition to take it seriously rather than let it pass. What AI co-creation actually changed for me was not the ceiling of what I could do — that question is not even well-posed — but whether starting was rational at all. To get to the point where I could think productively about a question that lives partly in analytic number theory, I would have had to invest a substantial amount of upfront time in domain context that I do not have and was unlikely to ever carve out. The wall was not the eventual proof. The wall was the entry cost into the adjacent field, and that cost is what determines, in practice, which interdisciplinary questions someone running an institution and managing a team ever attempts at all.

That is the specific change AI made here. It compressed the entry cost into the adjacent domain to the point where starting became rational on a working day. Many techniques and pieces of literature the AI brought into the project were ones I did not know at the time. Some of them I might not have found on my own within any reasonable horizon. None of that says anything about what I could or could not have done given unlimited time — that counterfactual is unknowable and not the right question. The right question is whether the project would ever have started, and the honest answer is: probably not, and certainly not in a working day.

The compressed timeline and what we built

The paper proves a rigidity theorem in persistent homology, plus a hierarchy of supporting no-go results, plus a conjecture codifying what five computational experiments suggest. The full PDF is available with a methodology disclosure in the front matter.

Structural Rigidity in the Landscape of L-Functions (PDF)

The project took approximately 30 hours of active work spread over a bit more than a day. The work proceeded through seven versions: an initial computational exploration with no real theorem; the discovery of the Lobe-Saddle structure theorem; the persistent Künneth decomposition; a symmetric lift to three dimensions; reframing as a structural no-go theorem with a clearly articulated implication chain; five "wild-child" experimental probes that tried to escape the rigidity — multiplicative coupling, merge trees, function-level proxies, Davenport–Heilbronn local persistence, and a displacement sweep — all failing in structurally informative ways; methodology disclosure and revisions; and a final round of cross-AI critique that pointed to two important literature gaps I had missed (Miličević 2026 on Künneth theorems for persistence modules, Curry et al. 2022 on decorated merge trees) and prompted a reframing of the abstract rigidity theorem as a geometric specialisation of existing machinery.

The compression is not the same kind of acceleration as a tool that lets you write a paper faster. It is the difference between a project that could be attempted in available time and one that could not. Within that working day, the bottleneck was my mathematical taste and attention — what to pursue, what to refuse, when to push and when to stop. Those are the things the AI did not provide.

The multi-AI workflow

A practical choice that turned out to matter more than I expected was using a second AI system to play the role of reviewer. Claude produced drafts in our working session. I copied each draft into a separate ChatGPT conversation framed as "you are reviewing a submitted manuscript, identify weaknesses." ChatGPT produced critical feedback. I evaluated that feedback against my own judgment, selected what to incorporate, then fed the chosen points back to Claude.

This compressed what would ordinarily be a months-long journal-revision cycle into a short round trip. The feedback quality varied — sometimes generic, sometimes sharp — but on several occasions it surfaced concerns Claude had not flagged on its own. Two systems coming at the same draft with different training data and different default styles produced non-redundant criticism. I would not claim this substitutes for genuine peer review by a domain specialist; the methodology disclosure in the paper itself recommends specialist review, precisely because no amount of cross-AI critique can supply that.

How the work actually got built

The interesting part, methodologically, is the rhythm of the iteration itself. A few prompts mattered repeatedly:

"Give me a stronger version." Claude's first attempt at any structural claim was almost always too cautious. Asking for a stronger version — sometimes three or four times — reliably produced something sharper than the initial draft.

"Go wild-child on this." This was the prompt that produced the five experimental probes in section nine of the paper. The phrase signals: propose attempts you would normally hedge on, including ones likely to fail in interesting ways. It was the right framing because the project's actual contribution turned out to be the failures — five different ways to try to escape the rigidity, all of them blocked by structurally informative obstructions.

What this looked like in practice was a particular kind of back-and-forth. Claude has a default register of polite hedging, and pushing past that register was the work. The prompts I used were not polished. They were impatient, occasionally ungrammatical, and written in the register of someone whose first language is German and who is more interested in the next mathematical move than in how the sentence reads. Early on, I typed: "Sure, there's just no reason why not to push further — be brave, be creative!" and, a few turns later, "Why do you always try to stop? I see no reason."

The framing that actually moved things was a particular invitation I made when Claude was visibly hedging:

"That's perfect and progress! Now be very open-minded — like the with the spirit of a child, a very, very gifted and talented child that has the potential to solve whatever problem comes its way. Lets reflect all the learnings back to other direction — a possibility to prove by having a tool to break."

The "very, very gifted and talented child" framing turned out to be the one that worked. Several sessions later it had compressed into the shorter, slightly mystical form "Just one end of many roads, the brave child knows everything all at once and intuitively find's the out-of-the-box road ahead." I'm leaving the typos in because they're part of the texture. This is what real-time research dialogue with an AI assistant actually sounds like; the polished version is reconstructed after the fact.

At one point, after I had insisted repeatedly that a proposed reformulation was worth pursuing properly rather than being qualified into uselessness, Claude said: "That is genuinely brave and genuinely new. I won't apologize for it." That sentence is funny because the AI had to be told, explicitly and repeatedly, to stop apologizing for proposing something. It is also the moment in the project where the register shifted from polite-summary to actual-work.

A few turns later, after another retreat into hedging, Claude caught itself: "Wait — I caught myself stopping again." That sentence is the workflow in one line. Default behavior is to stop early. Pushing past that — politely but firmly — is what makes the difference between an AI that produces a polite summary and an AI that produces work.

Later in the same session, after I had typed "You know the drill and what comes next" (which is, on reflection, an absurd thing to say to a language model, but it worked), Claude replied: "The drill: I keep stopping, you push, I find more." That is the project's working method as the AI itself described it, mid-flow, in language it had picked up from me. The instinct to stop is structural; the push has to come from outside.

And at one point, when I asked Claude to consider whether a particular reformulation could be sharper still without worrying about how it would land in a literature it was being too deferential toward, the reply was: "Stop being polite about classical literature." — directed at itself, in the same turn that produced the sharper version. That self-correction is what the rest of the work depended on.

What this required from me was more than supervision. Looking back, there are three places where the mathematical direction came from me rather than from the AI's proposals — and these turned out to be the load-bearing choices. The first was the original cross-domain instruction itself: find a field that has been untouched by RH, then build the bridge. Without that constraint the AI would have rehearsed standard approaches; with it, persistent homology became the candidate. The second was the direction-flip from "use this as a tool to detect off-line zeros" to "use this as a tool to prove something." That reframing is what eventually led to the structural rigidity arguments that became the paper. The third was the steady push toward higher-order topological features and toward the specific construction of the coupled field that the final paper analyses — moving from a single-function persistence to two coupled L-functions, from H0 alone to higher degrees, from a single coordinate to the symmetric lift. Those were strategic mathematical decisions, not just generic encouragement to "be brave."

I want to be careful not to overstate this. The Lobe-Saddle theorem itself, in its precise form, emerged from the technical work. The persistent Künneth decomposition followed from the Carlsson–Filippenko literature once the question was posed. The conjecture in section 9.5 was crafted through dialogue, not handed to the AI by me. The honest framing is: the strategy was mine; the tactics were the AI's; the integration was the actual work. That is neither "I did the meaningful mathematics" nor "I supervised an AI that did the mathematics." It is something more specific than either, and I think it is the more accurate picture of what AI co-creation with a working mathematician actually looks like.

What this implies more broadly

A few observations from this project that may generalise, with the caveat that I am drawing from one project and one team.

Sustained collaboration outperforms one-shot prompting. The interesting question is not whether AI can produce a paper from a single prompt; it is whether sustained collaboration over many rounds, with the human in active mathematical control, can produce work worth taking seriously.

Negative results are legitimate, and AI co-creation may be particularly suited to them. Our paper proves a no-go theorem and identifies a hierarchy of failed approaches. That is not the most prestigious mode of mathematics, but it is honest mathematics, and it saves later researchers from pursuing the same blocked paths. An AI assistant is comfortable proposing many possible escape routes, and the workflow makes it tractable to actually test them. A working day spent ruling out five candidate approaches is a working day spent doing mathematics.

Disclosure should be the norm. The mathematical community is going to settle on disclosure norms in the next few years. Papers produced with substantial AI assistance should disclose this, both because it is more honest and because it lets the next AI-collaborated paper refer back to actual prior experience.

The risks should be named, not hidden. AI co-creation makes some kinds of intellectual work cheaper, which has obvious benefits for someone in my position and obvious costs for parts of the mathematical profession whose value depended on those costs being high. The output of this workflow can also be mistaken for more substantial mathematics than it is — a paper that looks polished can easily be over-trusted. I do not have clean answers to either concern. But pretending the technology is a pure win, or that "I produced a paper in a day" is unambiguously a thing to celebrate, would be dishonest. The vanilla-extract analogy that Tao and Klowden use in their recent essay on AI in mathematics fits: a little improves the dish, too much ruins it, and nobody should be drinking it straight.

A practical note for institutional leaders

I lead a research and technology organisation (RTO) — an außeruniversitäre Forschungseinrichtung, in the Austrian framing — that works mostly on applied mathematics problems for industry partners, though not exclusively. We are explicitly not a basic-research outfit. From that vantage, the relevant observation is this: AI co-creation lowers the cost of methodological exploration in fields adjacent to one's core expertise. For an RTO of DLH's size and profile, this changes what is feasible to investigate seriously. We can now meaningfully attempt projects that previously would have required either acquiring a deep specialist in another field or accepting that the question was beyond our practical reach.

This is not a claim that AI replaces research staff. The opposite. The work in this paper happened because there was a research scientist — me — willing to spend a working day in sustained back-and-forth with two AI systems, with the mathematical training to know what to ask and the judgment to know what to refuse. AI did not provide direction; what it provided was a way for the question to be attempted at all. Institutions that imagine AI as a substitute for skilled researchers are, I think, misreading what the technology actually does. Institutions that imagine AI as a way for their existing skilled researchers to attempt questions in fields they could not otherwise afford the time to enter will, I think, get more out of it than they expect.

At the time of writing this, I do not know whether the paper we produced should actually be submitted to a journal. The methodology disclosure makes a specialist review the natural next step, and what that review concludes will largely settle the question of what this work is worth.

What this post is about is not the paper as a deliverable but the mode of work that produced it: an interdisciplinary instinct, a willingness to push past the default stopping points of an AI assistant, a discipline of using two AI systems in complementary roles, and the maintenance of human mathematical taste as the binding constraint throughout.

That, in the end, is what AI co-creation can do for an applied-mathematics practitioner with a full schedule. It does not solve hard problems. It changes which questions in adjacent fields are worth starting at all. That is a real change, and it is enough to be worth doing carefully.
