In 2023, I moved into a house pre-wired with KNX from day one. At first, I thought that was enough. KNX is reliable — lights, blinds, heating — and it needs no server. But within a few months it became clear: static logic baked into ETS can't use external data. No weather, no electricity prices, no cross-device optimisation. That was the starting point.

The problem with "smart" devices

The smart home market promises easy setup: buy a device, install the app, done. But behind that simplicity hides a trap. Every vendor ships their own app. Cross-device automation is difficult or impossible. And when the cloud server shuts down — as happened with Ikea's Tradfri gateway or Wink's forced subscription model — things simply stop working. Data leaves your home, devices become bricks, subscriptions appear. That's not a smart home, it's vendor lock-in with light bulbs.

The goal: local control, no dependencies

The alternative approach: full local control, cross-system automation, energy-aware decisions, no subscription.

Smart isn't cloud-connected — it's locally controlled, interoperable, and failure-resistant.

System architecture: four layers

The system is structured in four cleanly separated layers:

  • Field Layer 🦴 — KNX bus with MDT actuators, Berker switches, and a Gira gateway. Reliable, deterministic, works without any server.
  • Integration Layer 🧠 — Home Assistant in a Docker container on a NAS. Logic, orchestration, and the interface to everything else.
  • Messaging Layer ✉ — MQTT broker (Mosquitto), also in Docker. All non-KNX devices talk to HA through it.
  • Energy Layer ⚡ — PV system, EV, heat pump, and EPEX Spot electricity prices, unified as HA sensors.

The core idea: decouple hardware from logic. KNX handles the physical layer reliably. Home Assistant handles everything else.

KNX: the reliable backbone

KNX is a 30-year-old open standard for building automation, evolved from three earlier protocols: EHS, BatiBUS, and EIB. It's not flashy, but it's rock-solid. Telegrams travel over a dedicated twisted-pair cable, separate from power lines. Response time is around 50 ms. The system works without any server — a light switch stays a light switch, even if the NAS is rebooting for an update.

The limits of KNX on its own: logic is static, baked into ETS at configuration time. No external data, no cross-room optimisation, no dynamic reactions to weather or electricity prices. That's exactly where Home Assistant comes in.

Home Assistant: the brain

Home Assistant is open source, puts local control and privacy first, has over 3,000 integrations, and an active DIY community. My decision against alternatives like OpenHAB or ioBroker was quick: the quality of official integrations — from Miele to Reolink — was the deciding factor.

Setup: a Docker container on a NAS, a persistent volume for trivial backups, and remote access via Tailscale without port-forwarding. KNX is connected through an IP gateway — HA reads and writes telegrams and uses the group addresses defined in ETS directly as entities. 

services:
  homeassistant:
    image: lscr.io/linuxserver/homeassistant:latest
    network_mode: host
    volumes:
      - /share/docker/homeassistant/config:/config
    restart: unless-stopped
    depends_on:
      - mosquitto

MQTT and serial bridges: the universal glue

Not every device speaks KNX. For everything else, MQTT serves as the universal publish/subscribe protocol. The Mosquitto broker also runs in Docker on the NAS. Devices publish to topic-namespaced channels; HA subscribes and processes everything centrally.

The secret weapon here is the Elfin EW11: a ~€25 module that makes RS485/UART devices accessible over Wi-Fi — no soldering, no proprietary gateways, no custom firmware needed. Any industrial device that speaks serial can be integrated this way. In my case: the solar inverter and the smart meter.

Reading the inverter and smart meter locally

The solar inverter comes with a proprietary cloud app, running on Chinese servers. The alternative: an EW11 connected to the RS485 port, Modbus RTU polling every 10 seconds, data lands directly in HA. Real-time PV generation, no cloud required.

The smart meter has a "Kundenschnittstelle" — a standardised RJ-12 customer port. The grid operator provides the decryption key by email on request. A Raspberry Pi reads the encrypted data, decrypts it, and publishes it via MQTT. Result: real-time grid draw and feed-in, voltages and currents per phase — fully local.

Dynamic electricity prices as a control signal

The variable electricity rate ties electricity prices directly to EPEX Spot (AT) prices plus a markup. Day-ahead prices are published daily around 13:00 and arrive in HA as a time-series sensor via the Nord Pool integration. Four price zones emerge from this:

  • Peak (> 20 ct/kWh) — defer everything shiftable
  • Normal (11.76–20 ct/kWh) — standard behaviour
  • Cheap (< 11.76 ct/kWh) — charge, run the washing machine, heat water
  • Negative (< 0 ct/kWh) — turn on every available load

Smart EV charging

An EV is the largest flexible load in the house — up to 11 kW. The HA automation re-evaluates every five minutes: if there's a PV surplus, charge current is dynamically set to match it. If the electricity price is in the cheap zone (typically 1–5 am), full-speed charging kicks in. Otherwise, charging is paused. Timing one charge session correctly saves €5–10. 

automation:
  - alias: 'EV Smart Charge'
    trigger:
      - platform: time_pattern
        minutes: '/5'
    condition:
      - condition: state
        entity_id: binary_sensor.ev_plugged_in
        state: 'on'
    action:
      - choose:
          - conditions:
              - '{{ solar_surplus_kw > 1.5 }}'   # solar mode
            sequence:
              - service: number.set_value
                data: '{{ [6, (solar_surplus_kw * 1000 / 230) | int] | max }}'
          - conditions:
              - "{{ price_zone == 'cheap' }}"     # price mode
            sequence:
              - service: evse.set_charge_current
                data: { current: 16 }
        default:
          - service: evse.set_charge_current
            data: { current: 0 }                 # pause

Reading legacy meters with AI

Gas and water meters have no electrical interface and cannot be legally modified. The solution: an ESP32-CAM running the open-source firmware "AI on the Edge Device". The camera points at the meter display, a TensorFlow Lite model recognises the digits directly on the microcontroller, and the reading is published via MQTT. Total cost: around €15 per meter.

Automation philosophy: five levels

Most smart home setups stop at level 2. The genuinely interesting part starts at level 3:

  1. Level 1 – Reactive: switch pressed → light on (achievable with KNX alone)
  2. Level 2 – Scheduled: turn on at sunset
  3. Level 3 – Context-aware: turn on at sunset, but only if someone is home and lux is below 50
  4. Level 4 – Predictive: pre-heat a room before arrival using cheap overnight electricity
  5. Level 5 – Optimising: charge the EV while balancing PV self-consumption against the spot price delta

The key enabler for the higher levels: a unified data foundation. Once PV production, smart meter, electricity price, EV state of charge, and presence are all available as HA sensors, an automation like "if price < 5 ct/kWh AND solar > 2 kW AND EV < 80%" is trivial to write.

Design principles

  1. Keep critical systems local — KNX keeps working when the NAS reboots
  2. Use open protocols — MQTT, Modbus, KNX: documented, no SDK needed
  3. Avoid cloud dependencies — every cloud service is a future single point of failure
  4. Build modular — one integration failure must not bring down others
  5. Ensure observability — log everything to HA; if you can't measure it, you can't optimise it
  6. Don't over-automate — manual override must always work, first-class

Challenges (the honest part)

System complexity creeps up faster than expected. HA updates can break integrations. The most important stability metric: if the house becomes unreliable, features get rolled back fast. The solution: anything KNX-critical is configured in ETS as a fallback. HA is enhancement, not dependency — the switches always work.

If you want to start this kind of setup: you don't have to build everything at once. A single MQTT temperature sensor is a valid starting point. Each layer adds value on its own — and the combination becomes genuinely interesting over time.
