A small research-and-industrial cluster outgrows a batteries-included platform — why we moved from OpenShift OKD to k3s.

When we started building our platform, OpenShift OKD looked like the obvious choice. It promised an enterprise-grade Kubernetes distribution with integrated authentication, an internal registry, Operators, Routes, monitoring, logging, and GitOps support — all in one coherent package. Instead of assembling a platform from a dozen independent projects, we'd get an ecosystem that already fit together.

We ran OKD for a good while. Then we moved off it, onto k3s.

This isn't an "OpenShift is bad" article. It isn't. OKD is a genuinely impressive piece of engineering, and for the right organization it is worth every CPU cycle it costs. It simply stopped being the right fit for our environment — a relatively small cluster running scientific and industrial workloads, with GPUs at the center of it. The GPU story ended up being the clearest signal: NVIDIA officially supports OpenShift, not OKD, and what followed from that single fact captured almost everything wrong with the fit.

What we actually run

Our requirements were never exotic:

  • Kubernetes as the foundation, with everything else portable on top
  • GPU compute for AI/ML and scientific workloads, on a mixed set of cards
  • GitOps management through Argo CD
  • Data and ML tooling: JupyterHub, MLflow, Flyte, KubeRay
  • Grafana and an OpenTelemetry-based observability stack

Almost all of that targets upstream Kubernetes first. That turns out to matter more than we expected.

What OKD does well

Authentication via OpenShift OAuth is excellent — arguably the single thing we missed most after leaving. The Route API is nicer to work with than hand-managing Ingress. The OpenShift Console is genuinely well-built, giving you useful visibility into the cluster out of the box — though its integrations with the broader open-source ecosystem have limits, as we'll get to. If your organization has standardized on Red Hat, OKD gives you a consistent, well-supported experience that's hard to beat.

None of that was the problem. The problem was the bill — paid in hardware, in complexity, and in engineering time.

Where it stopped fitting

The friction we hit maps onto a few principles we now try to make explicit before adopting any platform: simplicity beats complexity, own only what you actually need, upstream first, and understand every layer you operate. OKD challenged each of them.

Day 0: getting it installed at all

OKD's install, for a self-managed deployment, is genuinely hard. The sentiment turns up regularly in the community — one r/openshift thread is titled simply "Trying to install OKD has the most difficult thing I've ever tried to do."

Before the installer will run: DNS records, load balancers for the API and ingress, and a temporary bootstrap node that exists only to stand the control plane up before being torn down. The nodes run SCOS (CentOS Stream CoreOS) — an immutable, image-based OS configured through Ignition at first boot; the root filesystem is read-only by design. Every "I'll just SSH in and fix it" instinct has to be unlearned. This sets the tone: this is a platform you operate its way.

The control plane was too heavy

OKD's HA footprint is three control-plane nodes at roughly 4 vCPU and 16 GB RAM each. Provision below that floor and a number of components simply won't start — we found this out directly. For a large fleet, dedicating that much hardware to the control plane is a rounding error. On our cluster, where every node carries real research workload and GPUs are the expensive resource, burning three sizeable VMs before a single user pod schedules was hard to justify. k3s collapses this: a single binary, embedded etcd, server nodes that comfortably co-exist with light workloads.

GPUs on an immutable OS

This one was close to a dealbreaker. NVIDIA's GPU Operator officially supports OpenShift — not OKD. OpenShift ships with a RHEL-based kernel; OKD runs SCOS with a CentOS Stream kernel, and that difference puts you outside the supported configuration from the start. Practically, this means NVIDIA provides no prebuilt driver install images for OKD — those images, which package the compiled driver and install it via MachineConfig onto the nodes, exist only for RHEL/OpenShift. On OKD, you are on your own.

We ended up building our own ostree-native container image that bakes in the NVIDIA open kernel modules and the necessary userspace libraries, pinning the newest available CS10 kernel at build time so the DKMS modules compile cleanly. A side effect: every node runs the same driver version — fine for a homogeneous fleet, a constraint when it isn't. The documentation and build instructions are public: github.com/DataLabHell/okd-nvidia-image.

On k3s with PCIe passthrough and the GPU Operator, we assign whichever card we like to whichever node we like, and the OS image gymnastics go away entirely.

The CentOS Stream 9 → 10 transition

OKD's SCOS base moved onto a CentOS Stream 10 foundation. With that, the low-level container runtime changed: runc is removed and crun becomes the default. The Machine Config Operator reconfigured every node accordingly during the upgrade, and the nodes rolled.

A change that far down the stack is invisible — until a reboot reschedules your entire cluster onto a runtime your workloads were never validated against. For us, the transition took out a number of internal components in one pass. The fix wasn't complicated once we understood what had happened, but understanding it meant peeling back several layers of platform automation to find a runtime swap we never chose. In a rapidly changing research environment, unplanned downtime has real costs beyond just the outage itself. With a batteries-included distribution, the batteries get swapped on the vendor's schedule.

A platform that abstracts away Kubernetes

Our team has deep Kubernetes knowledge — and most of it simply didn't apply to OKD. The platform adds its own abstraction layer thick enough that knowing Kubernetes well didn't help us debug OpenShift.

Problems would surface and the investigation would lead to pods we didn't recognize — platform internals we knew existed but couldn't easily reason about. Before we had scheduled a single research workload, OKD had already deployed around 30 namespaces and roughly 400 pods across our control planes and workers. All of that was the platform running itself.

The Operator pattern compounds this. An Operator enforces its author's opinion through a reconciliation loop: when your requirements match, it's magic; when they differ, you're arguing with a control loop that will patiently undo your changes. The sharpest day-to-day version of this was Security Context Constraints — most community Helm charts assume they can run the way upstream Kubernetes allows, and under OKD's default SCCs they couldn't. In a research environment the software landscape changes constantly; trying a new tool should take minutes. With OKD, deploying experimental software meant a recurring tax: patch securityContext, rework runAsUser and fsGroup, or author a custom SCC — for software that ran unmodified everywhere else.

Our cluster serves internal users only. We value flexibility in how the cluster looks and behaves over strict opinions about how it should — while still enforcing what matters: access control through Azure group permissions, restricted namespaces per team, and other Kubernetes best practices. OKD's defaults encode assumptions about org size that simply don't apply to us.

Components we never used

The internal registry, ImageStreams, the build subsystem, large parts of the integrated monitoring and logging stack — all consuming CPU, memory, and attention. Every idle component is something that can break, something that shows up in logs, something that needs to be reasoned about during an upgrade, something whose CVEs you now own. Idle features aren't free; they're standing complexity.

What our research workloads genuinely required was a short list: Kubernetes, GitOps, storage, networking, monitoring, and authentication. Everything else could be added deliberately, if and when we needed it.

What is actually open source?

OKD markets itself as the open-source upstream of OpenShift, and that's broadly true — but the boundary wasn't always clear. Open Data Hub, the service mesh, and others exist in open-source form, but the fully integrated experience often requires a Red Hat subscription, and it's not immediately obvious which tier you're on. For a research organization where open-source provenance matters on principle and for reproducibility, that ambiguity was friction we hadn't anticipated.

What we run now

Moving off OKD forced us to be explicit: own only what you actually need, understand everything you run, and keep every component independently replaceable. In brief: k3s HA on a heterogeneous mix of bare-metal and Proxmox nodes, Ansible for node management, PCIe passthrough with the NVIDIA GPU Operator, Argo CD for GitOps, the LGTM observability stack, and our ML tooling — JupyterHub, MLflow, Flyte, KubeRay — all running as plain upstream Helm charts, without security context patches.

The difference from OKD is felt every day: documentation applies without translation, Helm charts install without fighting the platform, and when something breaks it's in a component we chose to run — not a platform internal we didn't know existed.

What we traded

Leaving a batteries-included platform means rebuilding the batteries. We stood up our own authentication, ingress conventions, certificate handling, and observability wiring — things OKD provided out of the box. If we hadn't already been comfortable assembling cloud-native components, that effort could easily have outweighed the savings.

The migration itself was easier than expected. We backed up the data that mattered, tore down every OKD component, and redeployed on k3s from scratch — restoring workloads as we went. The whole thing took a few days. And because most of our software targets upstream Kubernetes, we could reuse the bulk of our existing configuration and drop all the OKD-specific patching we'd accumulated.

Every abstraction has a cost — the question is whether it solves your problems. For our research environment, OKD's abstractions solved problems we didn't have while creating complexity we didn't need.

Final thoughts

We don't regret running OKD. It taught us an enormous amount about Kubernetes, Operators, security, and what enterprise platform engineering actually involves. But our priorities today are different: we value simplicity over completeness, prefer upstream projects over platform-specific integrations, and would rather spend our engineering time on our GPU workloads and research applications than on another layer of platform abstraction.

For our environment, moving from OKD to k3s was the right call.

It won't be the right call for everyone — but it was the right one for us.
